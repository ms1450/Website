Journal 6: Cloning Technologies

First, research and describe the status of human cloning as an emerging medical technology (within the last 5 years) and discuss its impacts and consequences (both positive and negative) for society. Next, Rent & watch The Island (2005). How close are we to a future where The Island is a reality? To what extent does our capitalist (for-profit) political economy support such an imagined future coming true?

Cloning, according to the National Human Genome Research Institute (NHGRI) is the process of producing a genetically identical copy of a biological entity. Almost 30 years ago, humans were able to clone a sheep named Dolly as the first mammal to be successfully cloned. People believed that cloning was the next big step in the medical industry, it could lead to the eradication of genetic diseases and birth defects. This consequently led to scientists researching human cloning.

Following Dolly, I came across several articles referring to the successful cloning of monkeys at the beginning of 2018, proving that the cloning of humans would be theoretically possible, however as this article and various other articles go on to state, human cloning in the current medical landscape is expensive and unethical. This is because human cloning is much more intricate as compared to other mammals, it would involve millions of dollars’ worth of research and experimentation, and it would require women to donate eggs and serve as surrogates for the cloned embryos. While researching human cloning, one important factor that might get overlooked by people is that the process of human cloning is not duplicating. A clone will never be the same person as the original, they would not share personalities, experiences, or morals. This also raises several ethical and moral dilemmas concerning the life a clone might lead. The environment it is raised in, its identity, and its purpose for existence might have a significant impact on its psyche.

I believe that while human cloning would prove to be useful in terms of saving human lives and making considerable progress in medical science through experimentation and testing, it would be impractical in the long term and devalue human life. ‘The Island’ directed and coproduced by Michael Bay in 2005 is a thriller dystopian movie that portrays a world where human cloning is used on a massive scale as an insurance policy.

In this movie, humans buy clones of themselves to harvest organs from in the event they sustain injuries. Although theoretically sound, this idea falls apart in its implementation both practically and ethically, sustaining a large number of human clones would take a significant number of resources. In a future where we have sufficient technology as portrayed in the movie, to be able to replicate and clone humans, it would be much more effective to quickly synthesize the damaged organ in a lab environment without having to deal with sentient human clones walking around the facility. Ethically, I would compare this to slavery, the human clones are designed to be just as intellectually capable as their hosts, yet they are treated as a product to be used by a third party. They are also locked underground and constantly monitored, whilst limiting their social interactions. This would not only take a huge toll on their well-being but also be illegal and immoral.

Fortunately, I believe that our society, even if in a capitalist political economy would never support what was portrayed in ‘The Island’. Logistically, it would be near impossible to covertly produce human clones and provide their services, governments have regular audits and inspections in place that are performed on medical organizations to ensure they are following proper conduct, moreover, it would be extremely difficult to have thousands of employees required to sustain such an endeavour and prevent any whistle-blowers. If this practice is publicly disclosed, the heavy backlash from the community will ensure that it does not continue. Such a practice might take place illegally on a much smaller scale; I believe that it would not be sustainable and the risk of maintaining such a practice would be too much compared to its reward.

While our medical technology every day gets a little closer to a future where human cloning is possible, the scientific research required, and its moral implications will ensure that it remains nothing but science fiction.
